Why You Should Keep a Training Log

   An accurate, consistent running log can be a very useful training tool. With a running log, you can look back over successful and unsuccesful seasons to figure out what worked and didn't work. For instance, you can look at the three or four weeks leading up to an exceptional race and know which workouts your body responds well to. Conversely, if you are feeling particularly run-down, you might look back and realize that you've run too many hard days in a row, etc. You can also compare similar (or the same) workouts from year to year and have a record of your improvement.

   Including bits of commentary in your log will remind you when you had a poor workout due to lack of sleep or diet problems, or might help you diagnose an injury if you note when and how the pain first started. Another useful thing to log (which I actually do not, I'm sort of lazy) is your resting heart rate (RHR). Take your heart rate when you first wake up in the morning, before you get out of bed. In a few days you will know what to expect your RHR to be. Elevation of your RHR can warn you that your are either not recovered from the previous workout or that you are potentially getting sick, before you notice any other symptoms.

   Finally, keeping a log can just generally make you feel like a badass. You can look back at all your cumulative work over the years and realize in a concrete way how much you have done to prepare for your season, and that feels good. Going into a big race, you can look back over your workouts and comments and feel confident, and at the end of the season you can feel good at reading over forgotten highlights. Here are a few things that I think are important as a minimum to keep in your log:

  1. Date: Of course...
  2. Duration: miles, minutes, or both, depending on how your training is laid out. Recording both allows you to compare pace, or else you can include that as a comment.
  3. Type of workout: hills, intervals, tempo, fartlek, recovery run.
  4. Comments: write down how you felt, if you ate something wrong, if you did something special (e.g. what the interval workout was), if something made the run out of the ordinary (a particularly hilly course, bad weather, or something exciting that made you run faster).
