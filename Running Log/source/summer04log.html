The 60 mile week at home felt awesome, no troubles at all. I'm lucky I had a job where I got to rest so much. My hardest week would have probably been 55 in the middle of the summer, I think the second time I did it. But since August the staleness went away and I think I'm ready to roll. Now it's time to settle into some real workouts and get some speedwork into my legs, and then wait for November. I might start picking up some light lifting again, depending on what we do at practice.

Sunday, June 6- Camp Pendleton Mud Run
Distance: 10k Time: 55:38 Total Miles: 7
Comments:

    Hoooooooly sh*t! I don't know if there's any way to describe this race! It was so insanely hardcore, and SO MUCH FUN! Seriously, it was like, steeplchase times a hundred. In fact, there was a bit of steeplechasing involved, but it was more like steeple the way the horses do it... So this thing is basically a 10k obstacle course on the Camp Pendleton Marine Base, just north of here. There are like half a dozen mud pits, water crossings, two 5ft walls (covered in mud) to climb over, a tunnel to crawl through, basically craziness all over the place. I've been trying to do this thing for years, and finally this year, Amy, Tina, Julie and Brigitte were all doing it so I signed up too. And it was so awesome!
    Ok, let me try to describe the course: The whole thing is one big dirt loop, and all of the obstacles are added by the race organizers sort of "on top". So we start off, and about 400m in, they started spraying fire hoses at us. I don't really know why, that didn't add to the race much, but whatever. Then we get to the dirt trail, and we start in, and within a minute there is a pit of loose sand that we had to crawl through on our hands and knees. Also a bit unnecessary, in my opinion, but everything got better from there.
    After that, there was a little one of those tire obstacle things, and then the first mud pit. It was about knee deep, so you could pick up your legs and still sort of run through it. Then there was just more running for a while; there was about a 3 mile long hill in the middle of the course, but only part of it was particularly steep. At the top of the hill there was a set of hay bales, and then just a lot more running as we wound down the hill. A couple more mud pits were thrown in there somewhere along flat stretches too.
    About four miles in things started to get crazy. There weren't any mile markers so I didn't know that I was that far, but whatever. So after the hill I come up to the first wall. It's basically just that: a wooden wall across the course, five feet tall, with a pool of mud leading up to and away from it. My first attempt at vaulting myself over proved unsuccessful when my feet got sucked into the mud, but I managed to not sink in and get myself over on the second try. After the wall, I ran a little ways on a trail, then as I turned a corner I started to hear, "It's three feet deep, no diving!" And when I can finally see, I realize that we have to cross what is essentially a small corner of a lake. No joke. It must have been like 40 yards of waist deep water that I had to wade through. I acutally tried swimming it for a little bit, when my quads got insanely tired (as if the previous mud and hills had left them fresh). On the other side, we had to come out this pretty steep, muddy bank. Right about there was when I first started to feel how incredibly dead my legs were.
    Like, I said, there were no mile markers, but I heard someone mention that we had about 2 miles left. I knew there were at least two more obstacles, and I figured I could just stick it out (I had been running hard, but obviously not racing, the whole time). There was another small hill at this point, which was sort of disheartning, because I didn't remember it from the course map. Then a second climbing wall... again a surprise to me. But after that, Marines were telling us that there was only one mud pit left, and about 1.5 miles, so I kept grinding. There were the tunnels to crawl through, in which I managed to pass this one woman that had been in front of me for a while--I had a vague goal to win my age group, but she looked older anyway so passing her was just sort of unintentional. The tunnels were on top of a hill, and going down there started to be more Marines cheering and saying it was all downhill.
    Well it wasn't. One last surprise feature was a short, steep hill that also seemed to escape the map. Of course, a hill by itself is so civilian, so to spice life up a bit, they put a couple of hoses at the top and made the whole thing a big, muddy river that we had to scramble up.
    That actually was the last hill, and coming down it there started to be a couple of spectators scattered about, so I figured I must be near the finish. I picked it up a bit at that thought, and a little more when some people started yelling to me that I was the third woman. I finally reached the last mudpit, about 30 ft long, with flags over the top so that you had to crawl through it on your hands and knees. And that was it. From there it was like a 50 ft dash (or stumble) to the finish. I came across the line exhausted, in 56th place overall, 3rd woman and 1st in the 15-19 women's group. It was only when I stopped that I realized that my knees were bleeding, and when I finally got my shoes off (I needed a Marine to cut off the duck tape), I realized that my feet were bleeding too. First time my feet have ever bled in a race. There were also 7-10 minutes of delerium in there, that I don't exactly remember, but by the time I was coherent again, all of my friends had finished and their parents were swarming over to take pictures. What a great day...
