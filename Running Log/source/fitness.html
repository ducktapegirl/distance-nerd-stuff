Letter to Men's team from Coach Taylor:

The topic today is recovery with a brief explanation on fitness. We will begin with the basics of the circulatory system, expand on the changes that take place when a runner gets in shape, and finally explain how the system recovers.

Cardiac ouput is how much blood your heart can pump over a given period of time. The two factors that make this up are stroke volume (how much blood the heart pumps with each beat) and heart rate (number of beats per minute). The more fit you get, the stronger your heart will become and the greater the stroke volume. Given that the cardiac output is going to be the same for a particular effort, if the stroke volume goes up, the heart rate will go down. If you can lower your heart rate for a given effort (mile run in 4:30 for instance), then it will be a lower percentage of your maximum effort, thus leaving room for improvement.

How do you make your heart stronger? Mostly by increased aerobic activity, but also anaerobic work. No one's body uses all the oxygen breathed in. In fact, most do not even come close. Our bodies are not efficient at getting the oxygen to the working muscles. To increase that efficiency, you need to do extended aerobic efforts (30-90 minute runs) consistently in order to force the body to increase the capillary bed to the working muscles. This increased capillary bed will carry the oxygenated blood to the working muscles from the arterioles and the waste products away and back to the heart via the venous system. How much is enough and what is too much. Everyone has a bio-mechanical threshold. Some breakdown at 50 miles, some can handle 150. You need to find yours and slowly build up your strength, muscle balance and muscle flexibility over the years so you do not get injured.

Given the time commitments at MIT and the typically low mileage high schoolers do these days, I believe 70 miles per week is a good goal for freshmen. 400 runners should cap their mileage at 45 and 800 runners should cap at 60 their first year. As the years progress, we can work together on how high your mileage should go. I have never had anyone do more than 100 successfully without injury the entire year. Doing 100 during the summer and cutting back to 80-90 during the year had been a pretty good formula for some. Another way to make the mileage easier on the body is to do your morning efforts in the pool or on the cross trainer.

When your body finishes a hard effort, muscle glycogen levels need to be restored. You only have approximately 1800 calories of glycogen in your muscles if you have a proper diet and you have recovered from your previous hard effort. You burn about 100 calories per mile. Your body prefers to burn glycogen (carbohydrates when you work at 70% of your max or higher. When your diet consists of primarily proteins and fats, you may take as long as a week for your body to naturally convert the protein or fat to glycogen. If you eat a complex carbohydrate diet (fruits, vegetables, pasta, starch), which should make up 60% of your total diet, you can replace the glycogen loss within 24 hours. It is best if you eat some form of complex carbohydrate very soon after your bout of exercise. Obviously if you do two or more hard days in a row or without sufficient or proper recovery, you will put yourself into a hole that may turn into illness or injury and at the very least will not contribute to good performances.

One way to tell if you have recovered is to take your resting HR. A good resting HR for a fit distance runner is below 50, great is below 40. Once you have established a baseline by taking your heart rate over a number of days (using the same position, ie, sitting or standing, same time of day, after an adequate rest time) you are ready to compare your HR on a daily basis. If your HR is up 10%, you should not train hard and make certain you eat well. If it is up 20% for 2 days in a row, you should go in the pool or a cross trainer for a day or two. This may be an indication that you have a cold before the symptoms have shown up. Do not do another hard effort until the HR is back at the base level or at least within a couple of beats.

Please let me know if you have any questions.
