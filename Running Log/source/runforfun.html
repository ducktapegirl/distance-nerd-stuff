Running for Fun - an Essay
Written: 10/1/03 Revised: 12/09/03

I originally wrote this essay as an assignment for my expository writing class. It's mostly edited at this point although I may decide to make a few more revisions along the way. I'm putting it up because I told some of my teammates I would... I hope the people mentioned don't get embarrassed... I'm a little embarrassed myself at some of the details, so bear with me. Just try to absorb the implications and don't focus so much on the story itself... all righty

      The impression that most people get about my running cross country is that I'm always doing it. This assertion is actually not true, but it certainly seems that way to outsiders (those that don't run), and even I start to believe it sometimes. My training happens like clockwork, a routine that is both dependable and comfortable. Six or seven days out of every week I put on running clothes and hit the streets, sometimes training twice in one day if I need to get a certain number of miles in. I have run on my birthday and on major holidays; I ran the morning before I graduated high school and in three different states during a summer road trip with my best friend. My family celebrates Father's Day by going on a long run together, and I cope with difficult moments in life by lacing up my shoes and hitting the roads. I have ditched classes in order to squeeze a workout in, and even once—just once—skipped a Rosh Hashanah service in order to travel to Stanford for a cross country meet. I think it is safe to say, then, that for all intents and purposes, I am running all of the time.

      Naturally, then, one of the first things I did when I arrived at MIT was go running. I gave myself a solid twelve hours before I took to the streets (eight hours of which were spent sleeping) and then headed out towards the Charles River. I ran alone for the first few days, until the MIT cross country team met for their first official practice. It began at a perky seven o'clock on a Monday morning—four o'clock California time—but that was an insignificant sacrifice to make for such a significant event.

      Walking to the spot where the team was supposed to meet, I felt as though I was just starting junior high again. I had planned out a specific outfit to wear that day: my favorite Nike Dri-Fit shorts, an old team T-shirt and my high school trademark, an Adidas sweatband. I also wore my mustard-yellow Iowa sweatshirt that my friends back home had learned to associate me with, but even that early in the morning it was already warm enough to do without it, and I walked across the artificial turf field toward the athletic center squinting into the bright sun.

      A group of girls—or "women" as collegiate runners seemed to be called—was already stretching and chattering away when I got there. I suddenly felt especially freshman-like, shy and new and unfamiliar with everything. I stretched discretely just outside the circle as we waited for the coach to show up with instructions, trying to pick out who was who based on pictures and names that I poured over on the website in the months leading up to my arrival. I recognized their All-American steeplechaser and the record-setting 10-K runner and felt a tingle shoot through me when I realized that these fast people were now my teammates.

      In a few minutes the coach arrived and introduced himself as "Sluggo", and mentioned that there was also an assistant coach whom we were to call "HBH". I thought to myself that it would be a long time before I would feel comfortable addressing the coaches that way, but the upperclassmen seemed at ease with the nicknames.

      After a few hours and a little too much NCAA paperwork, we were finally given our running assignments. "High mileage veterans" were to run seven miles, "low mileage veterans and high mileage rookies" were to set out for six. It felt odd to be considered a "rookie" again, having four years of competition in my legs and having been a team captain on my high school team the year before, but I tried my best to get used to my new place.

      With the workouts given, the herd of thirty or so runners set off. Right away, I noticed a stark contrast between my old team and this one. In high school, all of us ran together on training runs, regardless of how fast we could race. Here, the faster girls ran faster and those who couldn't keep up were strung out behind. In a typical example of my tendency to overanalyze, I concluded that I should run with the second pack, in order to show my abilities but not give off the impression that I was being a conceited freshman.

      Soon enough, though, I tripped upon the obvious. It didn't matter that I did not know anyone on the team. It didn't matter that I was in a different state and it didn't matter what my position was in relation to other people. I was running. That was something I knew how to do, regardless of surroundings or circumstances. I had accumulated thousands of miles over the years, and the next six that I had ahead of me would be simply more of the same. I relaxed a bit, which ironically caused me to run faster. Soon I was on the heels of the four runners up front, rolling along with the Martha and the Julia and the other older girls whom I had admired from such a distance. They asked my name and I told them. Then the group of them introduced themselves, unaware that I already knew who each of them was. I turned around half a mile before they did and ran back alone. On the way back I was at ease, engulfed by the simple movements of running, looking around and absorbing the scenery that I realized would eventually become familiar.

      By the time I got back to campus I actually did feel like a newcomer, but not in the same way as before. I felt suddenly refreshed, like I had just recently encountered this world of running and was reveling in its amazing qualities. Over the next week, practicing with my new team, I found out all over again that I was hooked on running. With everything that was new and different in my life, cross country was the one element that remained relatively unchanged. The workouts were no more than slight variations from the ones I had done at home; I knew all the drills and stretches and extra exercises. As we ran loops around the Charles River, avoided cars in the streets of Boston and explored local parks such as the Emerald Necklace and Boston Common, I took in the sights of my new neighborhood and learned to appreciate it in ways that I never would have had I confined myself to the meager orientation week activities. We did speed workouts and I found the joy in running fast for no particular reason at all, taking me back to when my friends and I would spontaneously decide, "I'll race you to that lamppost." And I made friends. I met other freshmen and went shopping with them, I met sophomores who sold me old textbooks and I met upperclassmen who told me all about college running and college life. I met people from several different states and many different walks of life, but all with the same wild obsession: the need to run.

      Perhaps it is this trademark compulsion, this notion that everything must be intense, that makes the idea of "running for fun" so intriguingly odd to people who do not do it. Or maybe it is the fact that something so simple can draw such a fan base that intrigues people and inspires in them a strange desire to yell comments such as "run Forrest, run" and "my grandma can run faster than you" from their cars as they drive by. Whatever the reasoning is though, it always seems as though there is a thick line separating those who run from those who don't, as if there are two worlds that may never understand each other.

      The irony, though, is that people do understand running. Even people that consider themselves non-runners are willing to agree that jogging a few miles has a magical power, a relaxing and mind-clearing effect that nothing else can give. With people willing to admit this fact, it still perplexes me as to why people would not enjoy a sport where you get to experience this euphoria every day. Perhaps the dissuading factor for some is the gap between an easy jog and a gut-wrenching workout. Some say it hurts too much, that they could never race three miles. I agree. Racing hurts way too much; for twenty minutes every week I subject my body to more pain than sanity tells me I should. But maybe that's just it. Maybe cross country runners are all a little crazy. I would be hard-pressed to find a runner unwilling to admit that. Like all true madmen, though, we wallow in our insanity. We run when the weather is lousy and when every muscle and joint in our body is aching. We run not necessarily from one place to another but simply to be running, and take pleasure in the fact that nobody else will ever understand—until they go crazy too.
