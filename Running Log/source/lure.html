The Lure
Written Mar/Apr 04, Revised 4/4/04

     I thoroughly enjoy being a distance runner.

     Maybe it's because of my shadow; the way I can run with it and how my feet come together perfectly with my shadow's feet every time they strike the ground. I like watching my shadow run next to me in the late afternoon, when my elongated legs make beautiful, cyclical leaps across the ground. I like running early in morning with the sun at my back and my shadow pacing ahead of me, when I pretend that the distortedly slender version of myself is an African elite runner, getting her day's workout in before the blazing sun rises and bakes the earth.

     Sometimes, though, I think it's long runs, the days when you go out for 10 miles and come back feeling like it was 10 minutes. I love heading out, looking forward to all the time I will have to schedule my day or sort out my life, only to discover, forty-five minutes into the run, that I have thought about nothing but more running the whole time. And I love thinking about running the whole time, thinking about my next race or my summer plan or thinking about just the sheer beauty of the act.

     On the other hand, it could be the pride I feel when the weather turns sour, treacherous, or downright spiteful, and I don some extra layers and hit the roads anyway. I don't know if I do this because of my mileage compulsion or simply to prove that I am not defeated, but either way, these runs are some of the most enjoyable of all. Those days when you say hi to everyone you see, simply because you're all in the same mess and because everyone out there is a runner anyway. During that time when I am outside, getting colder than is usually healthy or being pummeled by precipitation that seems brutally unnatural, I feel as though humans can be a part of nature, rather than at war with it, if only we agree to lose some minor battles along the way.

     Perhaps it's because I get joy out of hearing the question "You run that many miles... at once?" or the chuckle I get when people feel compelled to tell me they once broke seven minutes in the mile in P.E. I love it when my friends come to watch my races--track races, of course, because watching cross country is a sport in itself--and I have fans cheering me on. Even more than the support though, I love jogging over to them after I have finished and seeing that they look more exhausted from watching the race than I feel from running it.

     Of course, the most likely case would be that I simply get a thrill out of running fast or far. I am fully aware that I am not extraordinarily fast or extraordinarily fit; at this point in my life I don't think I even make the "elite" lists for high school girls, but it's all about perspective anyway. To think that in less than six minutes I can be a mile away from where I started is pretty cool, as is knowing that I could effortlessly encircle the entire city of Boston on foot. It's a power trip too, knowing that I can do all of this on my own, anytime, anywhere, in any fashion that I choose.

     Yeah, I definitely enjoy being a distance runner.

     But at the same time, being a distance runner isn't always so glorious. I definitely spend a lot of time wondering what my life would be like without running. Distance running is about wondering if you would be happier without the sacrifices or if you could be that much better at the other things you do, but knowing that you'll never find out that answer because you're too afraid to see what happens if you stop running. It's about the doubts you have after a bad race or an exceptionally rough season, about thinking that all of this is ludicrous and that you should balance your life a little more and that someone as committed as you should really be much, much faster. It's about thinking all of this and deciding to relax and then realizing that to you, high mileage is relaxing.

     So that's what it is to me. Distance running in a nutshell, or rather, a brief list. I'm not going to sum it up by saying that running is my life, or anything like that. Running is not my life; I am not solely a runner. But I will be the first to admit that I am caught up in running to the point of obsession. It's so easy, with running, to become engulfed, because there are so many details, so many facets and motivations. A person can run for fitness or competitiveness, for camaraderie or for solitude, to gain energy or to relax. A person can run without thinking or a person can pour over publication after publication to find the right workouts, the right diets and all of the right extras. Or a person can do all of these things, picking and choosing each day why he or she feels like running and each run inspires a new reason to do it the next day.
