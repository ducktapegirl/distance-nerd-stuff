Alisha's Thoughts on Training 

   So I'm no expert on training; I'm not a coach or a professional runner or even a longtime runner if it really comes down to it. But I've gone to a lot of clinics and read a lot and spent a lot of time just plain thinking, and I've come up with a few general principles of training that I like to follow, and I figured that I might as well post them, since this is my website.

     Before I go too far, though, I would like to preface this with my belief that above all, you should listen to your coach (if you have one). I realize that there are some coaches who know absolutely nothing about running, in which case, I will respect the decision to deviate from his or her plan, but if your coach has half a brain I think it is very important to listen to him or her and truly buy into the program. I say this only because running has such a mental aspect, and believing in what your are doing can have a huge positive impact on the results. With that being said, my thoughts:

I think that training can be broken down into two parts: the running part and the extras part, and I will talk about each part separately.

Running
     Obviously the largest part of the training aspect. Long story short, in order to be good at running you have to run. Weightlifting, plyometrics, pool workouts, etc. are all beneficial to your running but for the most part, running should make a vast majority of one's training. Of course, there's the age old debate of quality vs. quantity, and while I can be diplomatic and say that neither one is good without the other, I must admit that I lean toward quantity. I know for a fact that increasing my mileage (even slightly) has improved my running, and I believe that it is beneficial to the most runners with moderate talent. Granted, there are certain people who respond negatively to too many miles and there are certain stages in life in which people should be cautious about their mileage, but unless increased mileage leads to injury or overfatigue, I can't see how running more could be a problem.
     As I said, though, quantity by itself is fruitless. Sebastian Coe once said "Long, slow distance makes long, slow runners." The distances I train for are relatively short. Even during cross country, when I am running a 5k or a 6k, the pace is never extraordinarily slow (we should hope). Therefore, in addition to the endurance that is clearly necessary, I think that all distance runners should posess a certain about of pure speed. In high school, even the girls who specialized in the 3200 meters (the longest track event) had impressive 800 times and could even eke out a solid 400 relay split. Therefore, while I think it is important to specialize, distance runners also need to diversify a bit. Overdistance training happens every day when we go on runs that are longer than our event, but I also think it is important to regularly run faster than we would ever run in a race. During the summer, for instance, doing strides a few days a week would make sure that are legs are moving quickly once in a while.
     Speaking of summer... well I'll make this brief because every should know that you have to run all summer to be good at cross country (and even track, because if you want to do well in track, you should run cross...). There are only two things that I think about when I run in the summer. 1) Start easy, but end up with volume. I start with what feels like a super-low mileage week in June and add 5 miles a week until I am 5-10 mpw over what I'd like to remain at for the season. Basically, when you add up your summer miles, there should be a lot of them. 2) Run hills. Running hills is key. It gets your quads strong, gets your heart pumping, gets you in shape. And who couldn't use a bit of help running hills? Notice I did not list pace. Unless you're really dogging it, pace is unimportant on summer runs. Use the summer to have fun and fall in love with running again. Make it easy on yourself. Run aerobically. The two things that I listed are absolutely essential, though, in my opinion. Of course, there needs to be a bit of structure to your weeks: long runs, tempo runs later in the summer, but the two things I worry most about are miles and hills.
     I guess that's about it as far as training goes then. Of course, the actual schedule of training and runs is much more complicated than that, if you're going to get into periodization and macrocycles and mesocycles and stuff. But my object here isn't to throw out some snazzy running terms and explain the spefic scientific background of running. There are plenty of books and articles on that. Right here I am essentially just writing out my feelings toward running, the fundamental rules of thumb that I follow and believe make someone a better runner. Of course, there is much more to training. Core strength is another big factor, (through weights or plyometrics, etc.) that is, building up your midsection and making sure your arms are strong enough to pump you through a race. But I think the most important thing is to run. Run a lot, run consistently and run hills.

Extras
So I think I've been pretty extensive with my thoughts on training so I'll keep this section short. Like I said before, I think the running is the most important part of training, but there are a few other things I consider important:

  1. Diet-- some people believe running is an excuse to eat whatever you want. Well, if you're running to stay in shape or lose weight or whatever, then I suppose this is true. But if you want to race well, I don't think you should eat whatever you want. Don't interpret this wrong; I am not an advocate for "dieting" or eating disorders or anything, but I believe you need to eat healthy in order to run well. The the classic "fuel in a car" analogy. Put healthy foods into you. This is not a discussion of weight or quantity of food. I am only saying to eat good food. Basically, the way I eat is based on how I feel. I also try to count my servings of fruits and veggies each day, and make sure I have five. That way, if it's after dinner and I am hungry again, I can count, "ok, I had raisins in my cereal, apples and carrots with lunch, spinach salad at dinner, that's only 4 servings so I should grab an orange." Or if I feel like I haven't had anything breadlike that day I might grab a granola bar in the afternoon. Dessert? I eat it. Eating dessert won't kill you and you shouldn't deprive yourself of happiness, but dessert should be a treat, not a habit. A reward, perhaps for a good workout or a good test score. Anyway, so I do think what you eat is important. And as much as I have been avoiding the topic, weight is a bit important as well. The problem is that people often can't distinguish between their personal ideal body weight and the ideal body weight that they see in other runners. Some people are built a certain way, and some heavier than others. At the same time, I think it is important to be "lean." For instance, if someone weighs 125 lbs when they are in top shape in the middle of a season, and they gain 5 lbs during an injury or offseason, I believe that they will be slower when they come back. On the flipside, though, losing 5 lbs below that 125 will not necessarily make them faster. That's just how I see it.
  2. Icing-- ice baths are wonderful. Several ways to accomplish this. You can go into an actual whirlpool, if you have a trainer and such. Or, fill up your bathtub and by those big bags of party ice at the grocery store and fill 'er up (apparently this works well if you stand in one of the garden trash cans too). Sitting bags of ice on your legs works too, as does freezing paper cups full of water, then peeling off the cup and massaging your legs. Perhaps one day I will look up everything wonderful that ice does for you. I know that it reduces swelling and also promotes blood circulation. Ice with your friends, and it can also make for exciting social time! Ice with your homework! Ice while you contemplate life! Ice is magnificent.
  3. Stretching-- I know there were some historical pro runners who claimed to never stretch a day in their life. Good for them. I stretch. Stretching is good for you. If you have no time to stretch, at least stretch before you go to bed.

So that's about it. I think I hit upon most everything, without getting too specific on anything. Like I said, I'm not a coach. I don't write workouts, I run them. But I think that every runner should be educated on what they are doing and should have certain policies of their own. These are mine. And if you want to get educated, check out my articles section--I'll keep adding more. Or read some books on running. Study your sport, know it inside and out. Be intelligent, because running is all about being smart. One more thing...keep a running log. I'll write about why you should keep a running log later on.
