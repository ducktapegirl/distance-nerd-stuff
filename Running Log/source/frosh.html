Summer 2005 Notes for incoming freshman:

Pardon my lack of formatting or style, we're going for content today.

Many of you would like to know what to do to prepare for joining the MIT women's cross country team in the fall. We are currently undergoing a coaching change and do not have specific information right now, but here are some answers that might hopefully be helpful. All answers are current as of June 4th, 2005 and will be considered correct until otherwise posted. Please email the captains, Liz Walker [lwalk@mit.edu] and Alisha Schor [ducktape@mit.edu] with your email address and any other contact information if you would like the most updated info.

Q. Do I need to come early for practice?

No, plan on coming for orientation or your pre-orientation program. We will be practicing at that time but don't require that you come earlier.

Q. Will there be cuts?

We don't know this yet. Last year, we had to make cuts for logistical reasons, because only 26 people can travel to an away meet. The time last year was 24:30 for a 5k. This may change depending on the coach we have in the fall.

Q. What kind of training should I do?

If you're finishing up a track season, TAKE TIME OFF. 1-2 weeks, whatever you need to feel rested. Before you embark on any training plan, remind yourself not to do too much too fast. Try to keep track of how much you run each week, and don't increase your mileage by more than 10% or so each week. With that in mind, here are some very loose guidelines:

Start running in early June, 4-5 days a week--try not to take two days off in a row. The length of the runs depends on what you can do (or did in high school); 30-40 minutes or more. By July, you should be consistently running 5 days a week, 6 would be best, and in August you'd want to be running 6 days a week, so it's not a surprise when you come out in the fall. In past seasons, we've run six days a week, sometimes 7 if we are trying to squeeze in a long run. If you're running 40-45 minutes, six days a week by the time you get to MIT, that will get you by. If you can't squeeze in that much, shoot for the 6 days a week part, and do shorter runs.

For those of you interested in slightly more specific notes, here are some details and general mileage goals. Low and high goals are listed, with the lower end being directed at runners who did not have a consistent training regimine in high school or did not run in high school. Higher numbers are guidelines for those who have been training consistently since 9th grade or so.

By the end of June, be doing a long run every week (7-13 miles). In July and August, you can throw in some "workouts." Run some tempo runs: 20 minutes "comfortably hard" or run out and come back faster, whatever you're used to. Also, run hills. You can just pick a really hilly route or do repeats. Those three (long run, tempo, hills) will take up three days a week. Do recovery runs the other three days, nice and easy, with your mom, your dog, whatever. Everything can be by perceived effort, and don't worry about pace at all on the easy days. As far as mileage goes, it really depends on what you did in high school. On our team the various training groups run anywhere from 30-50+ miles per week (assuming no injuries or other restrictions) during the school year. If you come from a strong high school background, getting up to 50-55 miles once or twice at the end of the summer would be reasonable, but if that sounds like a lot, don't be intimidated. If you want to run more than that, please do, just listen to your body.

Lastly, if you could keep track of how much you ran over the summer and bring that with you in the fall, that would be great. Just a general idea of miles per week or month, etc. would be enough. You can see one of the captain's training log, if you're curious, but yours doesn't have to be anywhere near as detailed or specific. This way the coaches can be sure not to over or under work you when you show up. Also, try to stay on grass and dirt, and make sure your shoes are not old or worn out.

Happy running!
