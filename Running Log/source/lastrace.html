Written 5/03/04, revised 5/07/04

     She woke up rubbing her hamstring; it had tightened up in her sleep and now throbbed with a dull discomfort. Digging her fingers through that chunk of flesh, she marveled at how well toned and powerful it felt. She imagined her legs as one of those skinless anatomical drawings, with all the fibers woven across each other in a perfect pattern and subtle bulges in just the right places. The runner had often viewed those muscles as something to be proud of, ever since she was sixteen and the slight hints of definition began to show through to the skin that was pulled taut over her calves. This year in particular she impressed herself with the way those legs developed under a greater mileage load and a regular weightlifting regimen.

     But that morning, massaging through the sinews and feeling the firmness in them, she felt sad. Sad for her legs, who had taken so much abuse and served her so well, who had absorbed week after week of speedwork and were now ready to fire it right back out in the form of The Race. Yes, that one race everyone hopes for at the end of each season, when the pegs fall into place, the barrel turns and the lock falls open. But that morning she was sad as she rubbed her legs, because there would be no Race this year.

     The season was over. In a two day whirlwind of sweating, shivering and 101 degree fever, the chance flew away. She tried; it would be discrediting to the runner to say that she lay in bed in an incapacitated state watching the opportunity sail by her window. No, it is true that with false hope this runner toed the line and eyed her competitors, sizing them up and deciding that she could beat them.

     She most likely could have beat them all, all the ones she saw at least, but in the heat of the moment she missed an opponent, one beast with a personal grudge. This was the same viral villain who sabotaged Thursday and Friday's training days, the one who caused her heart to thump at 70 shallow beats every minute while the runner sat entirely relaxed on the bus ride over. The same foe who made her muscles tremble with weakness as she lay under the bleachers awaiting her race, the one who made her breathing unnecessarily labored. But this competitor was not noticed by the runner, or more accurately, not acknowledged, because it was doubtful that sure a creature could have checked in with the runner unaware.

     "Runners to your mark."

     She stepped to the line and just before the pistol cracked she saw it. But there wasn't enough time for fear to grow in her, for suddenly she was off the line, gliding into position on the rail, while the cloud of smoke dissipated above her. Everyone was screaming, but the runner noticed nothing anymore except for the opportunity she was chasing, and so she called up the plan in her mind and went after it.

     And for a while it seemed as though the unseen opponent was not going to make it that day. The runner came through one lap, two laps, just under pace. Four laps--a mile--went by and things still looked bright. But the race was nearly two miles long, and that was a whole different realm. The last three laps of the race belonged to the other competitor, who was much stronger that day.

     It was over with 1200 meters to go. The runner was so busy with her immediate battle that the opportunity raced ahead uncaught. As the finish line passed under her for the last time, she knew it was the last time that season. Her shoulders heaved with a mixture of exhaustion and tears. She walked a lonely cooldown (the first one she had ever walked in her life) and felt incredibly defeated, not just by the race but by life. Her physical drain only compounded her frustration and disappointment, blowing everything out of proportion.

     The next day only made it worse, because the next day all trace of illness was gone, and it was harder to understand what had happened. The race results were published and the performance was etched, unrevokable. The qualifying mark was gone and having been sick only seemed like an excuse to explain away why the runner couldn't put it together.

     And two days later, she woke up rubbing her hamstring.

     It was still sore from Saturday's battle, and the memory was still fresh in her mind. She worked out the tightness in her legs and felt sorry for them, children stuck inside on a rainy day. They wanted to run, they wanted to push their limit and play chicken with their biological cycles, daring the lactic acid to do its worst. The hamstrings wanted to fire away, to employ their powerful pull and move the runner forward. The mighty "quads" were ready to drive forward with as much force as possible, her feet wanted to slide into spikes and her calves wanted to play their subtle role in flicking at the last second to get the final "oomph" into her stride. But The Race would not come this year.

     The runner didn't know what to do. This was not a scenario she had planned for or mentally envisioned. It was a complete surprise, a situation that she did not know how to cope with. It wasn't a matter of self-pity, she knew that she was not unique in her position and she knew it could have been much worse. But neither these facts nor the fact that she had "three more years" were any consolation. She didn't want three more years, she wanted one more race. One more chance to see what those legs could do, right then and there.

     Finally, she decided to do what she always did when she didn't know what to do: go running. The decision to postpone her post season break might not have been entirely therapeutic. She told her teammates that she was offering to keep them company, which of course she was, but in a not-so-hidden crevice of her mind there was a shred of denial, a little sliver of some unfounded hope that maybe if she kept running and kept in shape there would be another race, that would be somehow magically conjured up. The runner knew this wasn't a real possibility, but rather than face that she decided that she would just run anyway. After all, it's what her legs wanted to do. Maybe if she felt inspired she might grab her spikes and go to the track late at night, maybe run something crazy like a 400 or a clockwise mile. Maybe she'd just go out and run a plain old hard mile for no reason, the way she used to when she was younger and would go with her dad to the high school track and play while he ran his workout.

     So the runner kept running, just because. She decided that she would run until she wanted to stop running, until "nature" had run its course and her legs were content to take their break. And then she would set her sights on a far off day in November, and hope that The Race could perhaps come then. But that was too distant a day to truly consider, so she immersed herself in the running and hoped that she could maybe forget about The Race for a while.
