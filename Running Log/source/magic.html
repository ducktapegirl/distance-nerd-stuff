Magic
Written: 11/30/03 Revised: 12/07/03

     No one was in the stadium. Perhaps this was because the gate was locked and I had to hop the fence to get in, but I was there to run, and runners don't know much about the concept of "no trespassing." So I was alone.

     I guess it wasn't anything special, my being there, having gone to school there for four years. I had spent countless hours there at track meets and practices, football games and PE practices. But this time things were a little different, this time I was an alum, and this time I was standing on red rubber instead of crushed brick. The new track that we had been trying to get for so long was finally installed, and I had come to see if there was any magic in it. I crouched to feel it with my hands and then stood up to survey the whole field. Both the track and the turf were brilliantly colored, so radiant and unfaded that it seemed unreal.

     Beyond the fences at the top of the stadium I could see cars parked and I could hear soccer practices being held. But still, no one else was on the track. I walked up to the start for the 100 meter dash. The lanes were clearly marked, one through eight, big white numbers with yellow shadows. I toed the line in lane eight, right under the stands so that someone looking down on the field might not see me, and took a stride down the straightway, the homestretch for so many races.

     After 100 meters I pulled up, and trotted over to the field itself to inspect it. Stepping off of the track I saw that it was actual fake grass, not carpeted Astroturf but a field made up of individual plastic blades. Nice for the football and soccer players, I thought, but I run now. To me, the field only serves to do strides on and to await events on. I turned back to the track and took my place at the distance runners' start line, just as I had so many times before. This time, though, there were no competitors, no coaches or parents, no starter's gun. It was just me, reveling in both the novelty of the facilities and the nostalgia of my not-so-distant track memories. It was only six months ago that I was racing in a Torrey Pines uniform, but already I was in an entirely different place in my life.

     I started to run.

     I wasn't running hard, just smooth, trying to match my stride with the sereneness that I had felt since I got there. My footsteps were silent; last year they crunched as every step slipped backwards a little on the gravel. Now I felt in control, as if the track was pushing me and encouraging me to do what was within me. I felt like I was in my stadium, like everything could happen the way I wanted it to.

     I finished my lap and walked back up the ramp to the fence. This time a car drove by as I clamored up the chain link, but even if they had cared what I was doing it would not have mattered; my business was done. I had come to see if the new track was magical, and while I walked back to my car I tried to determine the answer . I was smirking with pride, but at the same time I knew it wasn't my track anymore. It certainly felt magical to be there, but why?

     Later on I realized. The all-weather was nice; it was quiet and comforting and thought-provoking, but there was nothing special about it. The magic was not in the track, the magic was in track itself. That magic had been there before, on the old dirt oval that flooded when it rained and was almost too hard to wear spikes on when it didn't. It was there on all the other tracks too, allowing me to run quarter after quarter, race after race, week after week.

     The track, flat and fast, without any other factors, is the purest test of a person's physical ability. On a track you are being tested on nothing but speed; there are no hills, no puddles, no changes in terrain. Some might note these attributes as what makes track boring, but in fact this is exactly what defines the sport. In a track race, competitors are there to find out who is the fastest, not who the course or conditions favor. Track is pure, raw speed, mechanical and unchanging. Strategy plays its part, as does mental control, but in the long run, the fastest competitor will prevail. Those who find track to be boring have not yet learned that the excitement lies in its simplicity. On a track one is able to observe unhampered form, perfect running strides, uninterrupted focus. Track brings people back to the basics of running, not disturbed by any external factors. It was this simplicity, I realized, as break ended and I headed back to school, that made track magical, no matter what track I was running on.
